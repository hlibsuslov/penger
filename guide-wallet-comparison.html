[ GUIDE / WALLET TYPES ]

Wallet Types Comparison

Hot wallets, cold storage, hardware devices, multi-sig — understand the trade-offs and pick the right tool for your needs.

In This Section
  1. Hot Wallets vs. Cold Storage
  2. Hardware Wallets
  3. Custodial vs. Self-Custodial

Hot Wallets vs. Cold Storage

Hot wallets are connected to the internet — mobile apps, browser extensions, desktop software. They are convenient for daily transactions but more vulnerable to hacking.

Cold storage keeps your keys completely offline — hardware wallets, paper wallets, air-gapped computers.

A common strategy is to keep a small amount in a hot wallet for regular use, while the majority stays in cold storage — like keeping cash in your pocket while the rest stays in a safe.

Hardware Wallets

Hardware wallets are dedicated physical devices that store your private keys in a secure chip. They sign transactions internally, meaning your keys never leave the device.

How They Work

  1. A companion app on your computer prepares the transaction
  2. The transaction is sent to the hardware device
  3. You verify and confirm on the device screen
  4. The device signs internally and returns the signature
  5. The companion app broadcasts the signed transaction
Tip Popular hardware wallets include Ledger, Trezor, and Keystone. Always buy directly from the manufacturer to avoid tampering.

Custodial vs. Self-Custodial

With a custodial wallet (e.g., exchange accounts), a third party holds your keys. This is convenient but introduces counterparty risk:

  • The exchange could be hacked
  • The company could go bankrupt
  • Your account could be frozen
  • Withdrawal limits may apply

Self-custodial wallets give you full control. You hold your keys, you own your assets — no intermediary can block access.

Multi-Signature (Multi-Sig) Wallets

Multi-sig wallets require M-of-N signatures to authorize a transaction (e.g., 2-of-3). This distributes trust across multiple key holders, eliminating single points of failure.

Common Configurations

  • 2-of-3 — personal use (phone + hardware wallet + paper backup)
  • 3-of-5 — organizations with distributed signing authority

Bitcoin supports native multi-sig via P2SH and P2WSH scripts. The trade-off is increased complexity in setup and recovery.

Air-Gapped Signing and PSBTs

Air-gapped wallets never connect to a network. Transactions are transferred using the PSBT format (BIP174):

  1. The watch-only wallet creates an unsigned PSBT
  2. Transfer via QR code or SD card to the air-gapped device
  3. The signer adds its signature
  4. Transfer back to the watch-only wallet
  5. The wallet broadcasts the fully signed transaction

Air-gapped signing provides the strongest isolation from network-based attacks. The signing key never exists on a network-connected device.

MPC Wallets and Threshold Signatures

Multi-Party Computation (MPC) wallets split the key generation and signing process across multiple parties — no single party ever holds the complete key.

MPC vs. Multi-Sig Unlike multi-sig, MPC produces standard single-signature transactions on-chain. This reduces fees, improves privacy, and works across blockchains that don't support native multi-sig.
