Operational Security (OpSec)

Practical steps to protect your crypto holdings from phishing, social engineering, and physical threats. Your keys are only as safe as your habits.

What Is OpSec?

Operational security (OpSec) is the practice of protecting sensitive information by identifying and eliminating vulnerabilities before an adversary can exploit them.

For crypto holders, OpSec covers three main areas:

  • Digital security — protecting your devices and online accounts
  • Physical security — protecting your seed phrase and hardware wallet
  • Social security — not revealing information that makes you a target

The question is not just "is my wallet secure?" but "who might want my keys, and how might they get them?"

Threat Modeling Basics

Before implementing security measures, understand what you're protecting against. Ask yourself:

  1. Who might want to steal from me?
  2. What do they know about me?
  3. What methods might they use?

Common Threat Actors

  • Opportunistic hackers — scanning for easy targets online
  • Targeted attackers — who know you hold significant crypto
  • Phishers — sending fake emails or messages to steal credentials
  • Physical attackers — who may resort to coercion ($5 wrench attack)

Physical Security Fundamentals

Your seed phrase backup is the most critical physical item you own in crypto.

Never Do This Never store a photo of your seed phrase on a phone. Never put it in cloud storage. Never type it into any website unless restoring a known-good wallet.

Best Practices

  • Store on a durable medium (metal plate, laminated paper)
  • Keep in a secure location you control (fireproof safe)
  • Use multiple locations for resilience against fire, flood, or theft
  • Consider a safety deposit box or trusted location in a different area

Digital Footprint Minimization

Every piece of public information about your crypto holdings increases your risk surface. Key practices:

  • Never disclose portfolio size on social media or forums
  • Use separate email addresses for crypto-related accounts
  • Never reuse passwords across services
  • Use FIDO2 hardware keys for 2FA instead of SMS (vulnerable to SIM-swap)
Pro Tip For high-value setups, use a dedicated device only for crypto that never browses general web content. Air-gap your signing device and transfer transactions via QR codes.

Key Ceremony Procedures

A key ceremony is a deliberate, documented process for generating and storing cryptographic keys. For high-value setups:

  1. Use a dedicated offline device
  2. Verify wallet software integrity (check the checksum)
  3. Generate the seed in a private location free from cameras
  4. Create backups immediately before going online
  5. Document the process for heirs or recovery contacts

Multi-sig key ceremonies require generating each key independently, on separate hardware, by separate parties. This prevents any single compromise from exposing all signing authority.

Multi-Layer Defense Strategy

Defense in depth applies multiple independent security layers so an attacker must defeat all of them:

  • Layer 1 — Device security (full-disk encryption, biometrics, locked BIOS)
  • Layer 2 — Isolated signing (hardware wallet or air-gapped device)
  • Layer 3 — Physical backup (metal seed plate in multiple locations)
  • Layer 4 — Social layer (limiting knowledge of your holdings)
The 25th Word A BIP39 passphrase adds an additional layer: even if your seed phrase is physically compromised, the attacker also needs the passphrase. Store it separately from the seed.
